Deleuze and Guattari introduced the concept of the rhizome as a model for knowledge that rejects hierarchical structures. A rhizome, like the plant structure it's named after, has no central root but instead consists of connected nodes that can branch in any direction. Knowledge organized rhizomatically allows for multiple entry points and paths, with no privileged starting point or authoritative center. This model better represents how ideas actually connect and evolve, allowing for emergence and unexpected connections.

Emergence occurs when a system exhibits properties that its individual components don't possess. In knowledge systems, emergence happens when connections between ideas create insights that weren't present in the individual notes. The whole becomes greater than the sum of its parts. This is why interconnected note-taking systems can be so powerful - they enable the discovery of patterns and relationships that emerge from the network of ideas rather than from any single note.

Viewing knowledge as a garden emphasizes growth, cultivation, and organic development over rigid structure. Ideas are planted as seeds, tended over time, and allowed to grow in their own directions. Some ideas flourish and connect with many others; some remain small but important. Dead branches can be pruned. The gardener's role is to create conditions for growth and occasionally guide development, not to impose a predetermined structure. This metaphor shifts focus from organization to nurturing and evolution.

Feminist epistemology introduces the concept of connected knowing, which emphasizes understanding through empathy and relationship rather than detachment and objectivity. Applied to personal knowledge management, this suggests that our understanding deepens through building connections - both between ideas and between our own thoughts and external sources. Knowledge isn't simply accumulated but is woven into a web of relationships that gives meaning and context to individual pieces of information.
