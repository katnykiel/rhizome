# Knowledge Management Systems

Traditional note-taking systems impose hierarchical structures that don't reflect how knowledge actually connects in our minds. We create folders and categories that seem logical at the time, but later we struggle to find information because it could fit in multiple places. This artificial organization creates silos of information that should be interconnected.

The Zettelkasten method, developed by Niklas Luhmann, revolutionized personal knowledge management by treating notes as atomic units of thought. Each note should contain one idea, and notes link to each other organically. Luhmann's slip-box contained over 90,000 notes and enabled him to write over 70 books and 400 scholarly articles. The key insight is that value emerges from the connections between notes, not from the notes themselves.

Digital gardens are an evolution of the blog format. Instead of reverse-chronological posts, digital gardens are collections of evolving notes that grow and change over time. They embrace imperfection and learning in public. Notes in a digital garden are interconnected through hyperlinks, creating a web of knowledge similar to how ideas connect in our minds. This approach encourages exploration and serendipitous discovery.

One major challenge with any personal knowledge management system is maintenance overhead. As the number of notes grows, it becomes harder to maintain connections and ensure that related ideas are actually linked. Manual backlinking is time-consuming and error-prone. This is where automated systems that can analyze semantic similarity become valuable, reducing the burden on the note-taker while still preserving the value of interconnected knowledge.
